## Aerosol Layer Height (ALH) by Sentinel-5P TROPOMI (Daily)
The Aerosol Layer Height (ALH) reveals the vertical position of aerosol layers in the free troposphere, pinpointing how high above the surface particles such as desert dust, biomass burning smoke, and volcanic ash are suspended. Unlike columnar aerosol measures, the ALH provides crucial three-dimensional context: the same aerosol plume can have profoundly different climatic, health, and safety implications depending on whether it resides near the surface or several kilometres aloft.

#### About the dataset

- The ALH is measured by the [TROPOMI instrument](https://www.tropomi.eu/) aboard Copernicus ESA's [Sentinel-5P Precursor](https://www.esa.int/Applications/Observing_the_Earth/Copernicus/Sentinel-5P) satellite, operating in the visible part of the spectrum around the oxygen A-band.
- The ALH is retrieved using an Optimal Estimation approach, deriving the pressure (and corresponding altitude) at the centre of a vertically localised aerosol layer from the shape of the oxygen A-band absorption feature.
- The measurements are performed once per day, providing near-global coverage of aerosol vertical structure for cloud-free scenes.
- The product reports the mid-pressure and mid-height of the aerosol layer; retrievals are most reliable over dark ocean surfaces and darker land surfaces, where high surface albedo does not obscure the aerosol signal.

#### Use cases
The ALH is especially well suited for tracking aerosol events where altitude is critical. Saharan dust outbreaks loft mineral particles several kilometres into the free troposphere, where they can travel thousands of kilometres across the Atlantic — the ALH reveals the elevation of these plumes and helps constrain their long-range transport. Major wildfires in regions such as sub-Saharan Africa, Siberia, or the Amazon inject smoke into the upper troposphere, sometimes reaching stratospheric heights; the ALH captures this vertical structure in near real time. For volcanic eruptions, knowing the altitude of ash and aerosol clouds is directly relevant to aviation safety, as high-altitude plumes pose a risk to aircraft far downwind of the eruption site. Beyond hazard monitoring, the ALH is a key input for radiative forcing studies — the same aerosol load warms or cools the climate system differently depending on its height — and for understanding how aerosols seed cloud formation processes in the free troposphere.
